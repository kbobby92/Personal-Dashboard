A Personal Theory

Clairvoyance

A Theory of Temporal Perception

What if clairvoyance is not supernatural — but a latent human sense, unlocked by trauma, that perceives information already radiating backward from events yet to arrive? What if the future is not ahead of us, but already here, casting its shadow on the present?

Part One

A Foundation in Experience

Before theory, there is evidence. Not abstract evidence — but specific, undeniable, personal instances of perceiving information that could not have been known. These are the anchors on which everything else rests.

Account One · Age 7–10

The Chicks

The Dream

Walking into the backyard. Newly hatched chicks in their enclosure — three or four colors, but the dark ones stand out. Then: drowning. Some chicks suffocate in their own water dish. Others lie mutilated across the yard.

The child wakes crying. Every detail remains crystalline. The parents listen — it was only a nightmare. Nothing is done.
That Same Night

Reality unfolds exactly as dreamed. The dark-colored chicks are found drowned in the water dish. Others lie mutilated throughout the yard. A child who had never considered that a pet could drown in its own water had seen precisely how it would happen.

Account Two · Age 30

The Sunburn

The Dream

Walking into Josh's garage. He stands with hands raised — the gesture of someone who's messed up. His body is covered in burns. His head, bald and exposed, is blistered and oozing. The image is grotesque and specific.

The car show happens the next day. The sky is cold and cloudy — not the weather of sunburn. No obvious reason to expect what's coming.
The Reality

Josh texts after: severely sunburned. Because he is bald, blisters cover his head exactly as seen in the dream. The specific vulnerability, the specific injury, the specific gesture — all matched.

Account Three · Age 30+

The Voice in the Dark

The Dream

Complete darkness — no image, no shape, no face. Only a voice. An old man, struggling to speak, forcing words out with great effort. He was trying to communicate one thing: that he was dying. The voice was labored, broken, desperate to be heard.

Upon waking, the dream was vivid enough to recount. The sound of the voice — its particular difficulty, its strained quality — stayed. It was described to a family member.
The Reality

The grandfather died days later. When the voice was described to the mother — the way it struggled, the effort behind each word — she recognized it immediately. That, she said, sounded exactly like him. In his final days, he had developed aphasia, a condition that robs a person of the ability to speak fluidly. This was something that had never been shared, never known. The dream had not just delivered the message. It had delivered the messenger — in the only form he could still speak.

Account Four · Late 20s

The Room

The Dream

A hospital room seen from above — the perspective of a small camera fixed in the corner, looking down. The room is dark. The entire left wall is floor-to-ceiling glass, and beyond it a violent storm rages: thunder, lightning, chaos pressing against the pane. In the center of the room, suspended in the air above the bed, floats a box — wrapped in chains that extend to every corner of the room, holding it in place. A small sink on the far wall, the kind every hospital room has. A bed beneath the suspended weight. No person visible. Only the room, the storm, and the thing that hung there — death itself, present and chained to every corner of the space.

One to two weeks pass. A group gathers to say goodbye to Amy — a best friend's mother, dying of cancer. They pile into her room as many as will fit.
The Reality

It is the same room. The window, the sink, the dimensions — identical. Outside, no storm. What the storm had represented became clear only standing inside: the world beyond that glass was chaos — grief, the uncontrollable, everything that could not enter. But inside the room itself was something unexpected. A calm. A bliss. A profound, still peace that settled over everyone present. The group fills the space and there is only one place left: the corner. The exact corner from the dream. Seated low to the ground, the recognition arrives fully. In the center, in the bed where the floating box had been, is Amy — her son to her right, holding her hand. The dream had not shown a person. It had shown what was already there: death suspended over the room, and surrounding it, an inexplicable serenity. She passed shortly after.

The Pattern

These are not isolated incidents. Multiple times per year, information arrives — sometimes as vivid architectural dreams, sometimes as spontaneous visions, sometimes as a quiet knowing: a compelling pull toward a subject or direction, followed by an event that makes the knowing suddenly coherent.

"Sometimes I cannot tell the difference between what happened in the dream and what happened in reality. They were so similar I cannot separate them."

Part Two

The Origin — How the Sense Awakens

Trauma and Dissociation

Trauma creates dissociation. This is not metaphor — it is neuroscience. When a child experiences severe, sustained abuse, the brain activates its most primal survival mechanism: the mind splits from the body. Consciousness fragments. The unbearable present is abandoned, and awareness leaps elsewhere.

This is not weakness. It is the brain's most sophisticated survival tool — an emergency exit from an unbearable now.

The Sixth Sense

Humans possess five recognized senses. But there may be a sixth — atrophied by civilization, overwhelmed by the dominance of sight, sound, touch, taste, and smell. In most people, it is drowned out by louder signals and never perceived.

This sense: temporal perception. The ability to detect information about events not yet arrived at the present moment. Just as photoreceptors in the eye evolved to detect photons, perhaps neural structures exist that perceive what we might call time particles — information carried forward from future events.

The Wave Model

But perhaps particle is the wrong metaphor. Consider instead: waves. Far out at sea, a ship — or a storm, or a collision — disturbs the water. The event itself has not arrived. It may be miles away, invisible beyond the horizon. And yet the ocean already knows. The waves reach the shore before the thing that cast them.

We are the shore. Future events are the disturbance — already occurring somewhere out in the deep of spacetime, already generating their waves. Those waves travel backward through time, washing over us before the event itself arrives at our present moment. Most people stand on the shore and feel nothing. But the temporal sense — awakened, attuned — feels the water move. It reads the shape of the wave and knows: something is coming. Not what it is exactly, not when precisely — but its outline. Its emotional weight. Its color.

This reframes clairvoyance entirely. It is not about seeing the future. It is about feeling it arrive — the way a sailor reads the swell and knows weather is coming long before the clouds appear on the horizon.

The Paradox of the Unlock

Dissociation creates a strange paradox. When consciousness fragments, the normal sensory filters weaken. But simultaneously, the survival instinct sharpens — the brain becomes hyperaware, scanning for anything that might protect the organism.

A dissociated mind in a state of heightened awareness can perceive what normal consciousness filters out.

In this state — simultaneously detached and hyper-alert — the temporal sense finds space to activate. The child's brain, forced to survive extremity, develops an antenna for what's coming. Not consciously. Necessarily.

Part Three

What Clairvoyance Might Reveal About Time

The Block Universe: We Are Stationary

We believe we move through time. Past → Present → Future. We are travelers in a river of moments, always moving forward.

This may be an illusion. The Block Universe model in physics suggests something radically different: all moments exist simultaneously. Past, present, and future are not stages we pass through — they are coordinates in spacetime, equally real. We are not moving. We are stationary. And events approach us.

The Shadow Analogy

Imagine standing still while an object moves toward you from a distance. The object itself hasn't reached you yet. But it casts a shadow on the ground where you stand. You see the shadow first — the shape, the outline — not the object itself, but the information it radiates ahead of itself.

This is clairvoyance. Events exist in the block, already real in spacetime. Before they arrive at "now," their shadows reach us first. Information radiates backward from their position in time. The person with an awakened temporal sense perceives these shadows early — they see the shape of what's coming before it arrives.

Wave/Particle: Information as Radiation

Just as light exists as both wave and particle, perhaps temporal information operates the same way — sometimes arriving as a discrete signal, a sudden vivid knowing; other times as a slow swell felt before it's understood. In either form, it is temporal radiation — information carrying the structure of future events, emanating outward from their position in the block.

The capacity to receive this is not rare. It is universal. Every person carries the antenna — built into consciousness itself. What differs is not ability but awareness: most move through life with the signal present but unrecognized, filtered out by noise, habit, and the assumption that such things cannot be real. The wave reaches every shore. Not everyone has learned to feel it.

Part Four

Implications of Determinism and the Illusion of Choice

The Future is Already There

If clairvoyance works — if we can perceive information about events before they occur — then those events already exist in spacetime. They are not possibilities. They are actualities. Already real, just not yet arrived at the "now" coordinate. This implies determinism: the future is not open. It is fixed.

And Yet — Choice Remains

We experience choice. We feel ourselves deciding. The sense of freedom is real, even if the block is already complete. How can both be true?

The shadow holds both possibilities. The event that casts the shadow will occur — but the information it carries includes all variables, including whether anyone acts on the warning it provides. The non-intervention was already part of the future being perceived.

We experience freedom because we are part of the deterministic pattern. We are the ones who choose — and our choices are already what they always were going to be.

A Theory, Not a Proof

An attempt to explain a real phenomenon — repeated, specific, undeniable instances of perceiving information about events before they occur — using frameworks from neuroscience, physics, and the study of consciousness.
